You asked whether to pivot or push. The original advice said: run a pre-buy test, define the pass bar in advance, handle the employee and co-founder questions, don't kill the current product — transition it. That framing was sound in its sequencing.

What the audit sharpened: the pre-buy test as designed validates willingness-to-pay in three people. It doesn't check whether 14 months of runway is actually enough to build the new product and close the first customers — that estimate never entered the action list. It also doesn't clarify what the three customers are signaling they'd pay for: a standalone pivot, or the workflow capability added to what they already have. That distinction determines whether the hybrid path is already what your own test is validating, without you knowing it.

On the customer side, the timing advice was partially wrong. Announcing the transition early helps avoid surprises, but without a pre-committed date when the old product stops, the transition has no mechanism to close — which is the exact outcome the advice warned against. Two instruments are needed: announcement timing (covered) and a hard sunset commitment (not covered).

The revised direction adds four items to the action list and corrects the sequencing on one. The most load-bearing additions are the build timeline estimate and the pre-buy test clarifying question. Everything else stays.

01

What Changed

The artifact opens where the memo opens: the useful output is the shift in the advice.

01

Add build timeline estimate to the 14-day window.

Before the pivot decision, the engineer needs to scope the build: time to a minimum viable version and time to first paid customer. If build plus sales cycle exceeds runway, the pivot requires outside capital first — and the decision is already made.

02

Correct customer communication sequencing.

Announce the transition when the new product is 60-70% complete, not at pivot decision time. Separately, commit now to a hard date when the old product goes maintenance-only or dark — without that date, the transition has no structural endpoint.

03

Broaden the validation to three tracks.

The pre-buy test checks three warm relationships. Add: (a) a one-question pulse to the other 19 current customers; (b) 10-15 conversations with dental practices you have not spoken to; (c) one question inside the pre-buy test itself asking whether they would pay for a standalone product or the capability on their current product.

04

Check the shareholder agreement before the co-founder conversation.

Her 15% equity may come with consent rights over strategic decisions. Informing her is necessary; it may not be sufficient. A legal check before the call.
