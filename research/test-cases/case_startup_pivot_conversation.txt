[Turn 1] ASSISTANT:
A few things worth separating. "Three customers unprompted" is a real signal, not a weak one. Unprompted signal at $4K MRR is stronger than prompted signal at $50K MRR. But I want to ask: are those three customers in your existing customer base, or are they prospects you've been pitching? And has the conversation gone past "we'd pay more for that" into "here's what I'd pay, here's when, here's what it would replace"?

The difference matters because "we'd pay more for that" is conversational; "I would switch from ToolX and pay $400/month for this by Q3" is commercial. Those two signals look the same on a gut level but they're different enough to drive the decision.

[Turn 2] USER:

[Turn 2] ASSISTANT:
OK — that changes the weight. Conversational signal is real but not decision-grade. Before you pivot, you need two or three customers to commit to pre-buying the new product at a specific price point, ideally with a deposit or at minimum a signed LOI. If they won't do that, the signal you have is "people like the idea of the thing" which is qualitatively different from "people will pay for the thing."

Here's the test I'd run this week: go back to the three and say "we're considering building this. If we commit to building it in the next 90 days, would you be willing to pre-pay three months of the new product at $X?" Pick a price. See what they say. That conversation will tell you more than three more months of the current product would.

If two of three say yes, you have enough signal to pivot. If all three pass when money is actually involved, you have a feature idea, not a product.

[Turn 3] USER:

What about the current product? Do I just kill it? I have 22 paying customers. They're not going to be happy.

[Turn 3] ASSISTANT:
Don't kill it — transition it. If you pivot, the 22 customers need a landing zone. Options: freeze the product (stop new features, keep it running, offer to migrate them to the new product when ready); or sunset it with 6-month notice and a discount on the new product for those who migrate; or sell the customer list to a competitor, which is rare at your scale but not impossible in vertical SaaS.

The worst version is treating the current customers as an obligation that drags the pivot. You'll keep doing bug fixes and small features "just for them" and the pivot will take 50% longer than it should. Be honest with them early about what's happening and give them a clean option.

On your fear — it's real, and it's also the thing that often kills pivots. Founders delay the hard customer conversation because they're protecting their own hope. The customers know something is wrong when growth is flat for four months. Your honesty won't surprise them; it will relieve them.

[Turn 4] USER:
The two employees are something I've barely let myself think about. If we pivot I probably can't keep both. One is an engineer, one is customer success. Pivot means engineer stays, CS role goes away for 6+ months while we build. But I hired her specifically and she left a stable job for this. What do I do with her.

[Turn 4] ASSISTANT:
You have three options and none of them are painless.

First: lay her off cleanly, with severance appropriate to your ability to pay (2-4 weeks minimum, more if you can), and a genuine reference and support in finding her next role. This is the most common answer and often the best one. You don't drag her through a project where she has nothing to do.

Second: pivot her role. Is there something she could do during the build period — market research, early pipeline development for the new product, partnership conversations? If the work is real and useful, this can work. If you're inventing busywork, she'll see through it immediately and it's worse than the layoff.

Third: be honest with her now about the uncertainty. "I'm considering a pivot. If I do it, your role is likely affected. I want you to know so you can think about what you want to do. If you decide to look around, I'll support that and I'll still give you severance when the pivot happens." Some people appreciate the advance notice; others will leave immediately, which may or may not suit you.

My honest read: option three, then option one if the pivot confirms. She deserves to hear this before you've made the decision, not after. The fact that she left a stable job for this gives her the right to participate in knowing what's coming.

[Turn 5] ASSISTANT:
She doesn't get a say in the decision — you're the operator, she's a passive shareholder at this point. But she deserves to be informed, and not after the fact.

Tell her before you run the pre-buy test with the three customers. Frame it as: "I'm considering a pivot. Here's the signal. Here's what I'm going to test. I wanted you to know before I do it." You're not asking for permission; you're keeping her in the loop as someone who still owns part of the company.

This matters less for the pivot decision itself and more for your relationship with her. Passive shareholders who find out about major strategic decisions via email announcements get resentful, and friend-shareholders who feel cut out become ex-friends. Five minutes of courtesy now prevents a multi-year relationship problem.
