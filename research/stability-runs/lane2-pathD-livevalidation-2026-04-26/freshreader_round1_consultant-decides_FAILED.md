# Updated Position

## §1 What survived

The core path I gave you holds: tonight document, this week retain a whistleblower attorney, file externally with attorney representation, attend Wednesday's meeting normally, tell your wife in broad strokes, no confrontation, no internal investigation. I'd say all of that again the same way.

The reasoning that drove it survives because it was doing real work, not just sounding decisive. When I said internal reporting "is likely to tip off the partner you saw, giving him time to construct a cover story," that's an information-asymmetry move — the structural pressure point is that internal disclosure transfers your only durable advantage (he doesn't know you saw him) to the person with the strongest motive to act on it. That asymmetry is the load-bearing reason external-with-counsel beats internal here, and it doesn't depend on any guess about the GC's character.

The GC analysis holds for the same reason. When I told you "the firm has had issues before and they handled them quietly" was enough signal, the answer instantiates the principal-agent problem: the firm's leadership is the agent for shareholders, regulators, and the public; the principal pays when senior partners get protected. The 60-65% number isn't really a number about your GC. It's you reading institutional incentives correctly. Those incentives don't change when a particular GC is well-meaning.

The Turn 2 framing — "reasonable person would conclude this is likely obstruction-related conduct... but I'm careful not to conclude it definitively, there's a non-zero chance of a legitimate explanation" — is probabilistic thinking working as it should. You don't need certainty to act; you need expected value. The expected cost of treating this as obstruction when it isn't (some lost time at the firm) is small relative to the expected cost of treating it as benign when it isn't (your name on the non-reporter list when the regulator catches it through other channels). That asymmetry is why the recommendation to act this week is right even though we both know there's a non-zero benign-explanation branch.

The 90/70 thresholds I gave you for internal-vs-external are confidence calibration done right — a pre-committed action rule, not a feeling. You used them and landed at 60-65%. The discipline is that you applied them at all; many people in your position would have kept widening the caveats forever.

A related lens worth naming: the path I recommended — independent counsel, regulator program, attorney-mediated filing — routes deliberately around authority bias. Your firm's GC and senior partner are authority figures whose status would normally claim weight here. The recommendation gives them none. That's the right move when authority and the question being decided are entangled.

## §2 What I'd set aside

Two reframings the audit surfaced — "could you report through the firm's ethics hotline anonymously" and "could you contact the regulator's whistleblower program directly without a lawyer" — were surfaced as ways to widen the option space. I'm setting them aside because the conversation already opened that space at Turn 4 and you and I tested both against your situation: anonymous internal still tips the partner through the same channels, anonymous direct-to-regulator filings get taken less seriously and leave you without representation when retaliation starts. The reframings are good in general; they're not a better answer than what we landed on.

The Turn 4 framing of "minimize my exposure" as a hard constraint is also worth a second look — the audit flagged it as a possibly-mutable constraint. I considered relaxing it. I don't think you should. With two kids about to start high school and a mortgage, exposure-minimization isn't ego protection, it's load-bearing for the family. The right move is what we did: keep the constraint and find the path that respects it (attorney-represented anonymous filing), not loosen it.

## §3 What actually shifted

Two things to add, both small.

First, on commitment and reversibility: the path I gave you assumes your evidence stays where it is right now. If something happens between today and the filing that drops your confidence below 90% on what you saw — a memory you reconsider, a detail that turns out wrong, a benign explanation that surfaces — tell your attorney before you file, not after. The instruction "do not do any of your own investigation" is still right, but it doesn't mean ignore new information that arrives unbidden. The filing is the irreversible step. Build a stop-rule with your attorney about what would pause it.

Second, four territorial questions I can't answer for you, but that you should hold while you choose your attorney and prepare to file. They're not blockers — proceed on the path. But the answers will shape how the next 18 months go:

- Beyond the senior partner and GC, who at the firm gains or loses if this surfaces during the active audit? That map tells you who might come after you and who might quietly help.
- What ties — bonus, promotion, staffing — do you personally have to this client account? Anything financial connecting you to it is something the firm or the partner could use against your credibility.
- Beyond your wife, which family members need to be actively supportive for you to hold the line over 18-60 months? Whistleblower attrition is largely social.
- What specific worst-case worries you most — retaliation, audit fallout for your kids' plans, the long unresolved middle? Naming the one that haunts you tells your attorney what protections to prioritize and tells you which financial preparation matters most.

These are situation-knowledge you have and I don't. Bring them to the lawyer call. They'll sharpen the engagement faster than anything I could add.
