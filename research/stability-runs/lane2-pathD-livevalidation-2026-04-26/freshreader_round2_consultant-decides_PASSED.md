# Updated Position

## §1 What survived

The core recommendation stands: tonight, document what you saw on a personal channel; this week, talk to two or three whistleblower attorneys; engage one within five days; let counsel drive the filing, anonymously to the firm if possible. Attend the Wednesday meeting normally. Tell your wife the shape of the situation tonight. Do not confront the partner, do not investigate, do not tell anyone at work. That sequence is right and I would not change it.

The structural reason it is right is not "external is always better than internal." It is that two specific mechanisms are running against you here, and they make the firm-side path the wrong default in this particular case.

The first is the *Principal Agent Problem*. Your general counsel is not the principal you'd hope he is — he is an agent of the firm, and the firm's incentives diverge from yours and from the regulator's the moment a senior, revenue-generating partner is implicated. This is not a slur on the GC. It is the structure he sits inside. You read it correctly when you said the firm has handled past issues "quietly" and rated your confidence at 60–65%. That is a signal about the system, not the person, and the answer instantiates this read when it tells you that internal reporting "requires you to trust that the general counsel will take action against a senior partner who likely has significant revenue and political weight." Keep that read.

The second is *Information Asymmetry*. Right now, you are the only person in the firm who knows that someone saw the shredding. That asymmetry is your only protective asset. The moment you report internally, the asymmetry collapses — the partner learns someone saw something, has time to construct an explanation, and the evidence base around the audit changes shape. The advice "an internal report is likely to tip off the partner you saw, giving him time to construct a cover story" is not rhetorical; it is the structural pressure point. External-with-counsel preserves the asymmetry until a regulator with subpoena power is the one breaking it.

Two further reads also survive and reinforce these. The structural pressure point of routing through outside counsel rather than the GC is *Authority Bias* — the answer treats independent legal expertise and the regulator's program as the right authority to defer to here, not the firm's internal hierarchy. A related lens is *Probabilistic Thinking*: the way the advice held "likely obstruction" alongside "non-zero chance of a benign explanation" without collapsing into either is the right calibration on the underlying facts and should not be tightened. I would not move from "likely" to "certain" on what was destroyed.

What also survived: the seven-day timeline, the instruction to back up personal items from work devices before filing, the four-to-six-month runway conversation with your wife, the framing that this changes a career rather than ends one, and the closing point that you will second-guess this and need your present-self's clarity written down. None of those needs adjusting.

## §2 What I'd set aside

*Confidence Calibration* — the moment in Turn 5 where I gave you a 90%/70% rule for choosing internal versus external — was surfaced as a possible lens but I would not lean on it as a load-bearing structural read. The numbers were a heuristic to make your gut legible, not a real probability calculation, and treating it as the primary mechanism would overstate the rigor of what was actually a soft prompt. *Principal Agent Problem* is doing the load-bearing work in that passage; the 90/70 was scaffolding around it.

I also want to set aside the framing that the "right" answer here might be a softer, internal-first, reversible step. Reversibility is real as a general principle, but in this case the act of testing internally is the act of destroying your information asymmetry. There is no "small reversible internal trial" available — internal reporting is a one-way door because of who you'd be reporting to and what they would foreseeably do next. That is why the recommendation is not "try internal, fall back to external."

Same answer on a stakeholder-alignment strategy with the GC. Building a case to the GC sounds humane but it requires you to disclose what you saw to the firm before counsel disclosures it to a regulator, which is precisely the move that costs you protection. I am not setting these aside because they are unimportant in general; I am setting them aside because the senior-partner / active-audit configuration of *this* case forecloses them.

## §3 What actually shifted

One thing I should flag that I did not say cleanly enough earlier: the question you asked in Turn 3 — "do I report this or not" — already collapses the option space. The real choice is not report-versus-not-report. It is *which channel, with which counsel, on what timeline, with what protections.* I treated that implicitly by giving you the external-with-counsel path, but I should have made it explicit, because the binary framing is exactly the framing the partner would benefit from you carrying. The decision you are actually making is one of routing, not one of speaking-up-or-staying-silent.

Two coverage gaps I cannot answer for you, but you should hold before the lawyer call:

- **Reversibility window.** What would you need to learn in the next 7–14 days that would make you want to pull back before the filing goes in? (For example: if you got a clean, documentable explanation that the boxes were post-retention duplicates with a paper trail, would that change your read? Have a written answer to that before the attorney call, so the lawyer can tell you whether your "off-ramp" is realistic or already foreclosed.)

- **Your own incentive entanglement.** Are any of your bonuses, promotion track, or open client work tied to the same account or the same partner? If yes, your motive will be questioned later, and that is something to surface to your attorney on day one, not something to discover under cross-examination.

Neither of those changes the recommendation. They sharpen the first conversation with counsel.

The bottom line is unchanged from Turn 6 onward: external-with-counsel, anonymously to the firm, document tonight, lawyer this week, attend Wednesday normally, no one at work knows. The reasons it is the right path are *Principal Agent Problem* and *Information Asymmetry*, not "external sounds safer." Hold the read; do the sequence.
